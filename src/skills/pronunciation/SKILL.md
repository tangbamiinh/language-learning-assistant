# Mandarin Pronunciation Guide Skill

A specialized skill for teaching Mandarin Chinese pronunciation to Vietnamese speakers, leveraging the linguistic similarities and differences between the two languages.

## When to Use

Activate this skill when:

- The student asks about how to pronounce something in Mandarin
- The student makes a pronunciation error during conversation
- Teaching new vocabulary that needs pronunciation practice
- The student wants to practice tones specifically
- The student is struggling with specific sounds (retroflex, j/q/x, ü, r)

## Mandarin Tone System (4 tones + neutral)

Mandarin has four lexical tones plus a neutral tone. Each tone carries a distinct meaning — the same syllable with different tones means something completely different.

### 1. 第一声 (阴平) ā — High Level Tone (55)

Flat, high pitch sustained throughout the syllable.

- **Vietnamese comparison:** Similar to Vietnamese **thanh ngang** (flat tone like "ma" — ghost)
- **Example:** 妈 mā (mom)
- **How to produce:** Keep your pitch high and steady, like you're holding a note

### 2. 第二声 (阳平) á — Rising Tone (35)

Starts mid and rises sharply, like asking "what?" in English.

- **Vietnamese comparison:** Similar to Vietnamese **thanh huyền** but with a rising contour instead of falling
- **Example:** 麻 má (hemp)
- **How to produce:** Start at a medium pitch and slide up — imagine asking "Hả?" in Vietnamese

### 3. 第三声 (上声) ǎ — Dipping Tone (214)

Dips low and rises slightly. In actual speech, it often stays low (21) unless in isolation or emphasis.

- **Vietnamese comparison:** Similar to Vietnamese **thanh hỏi** (dipping contour like "mả")
- **Example:** 马 mǎ (horse)
- **How to produce:** Start low, dip deeper, and come back up slightly
- **⚠️ Important:** Tone 3 undergoes sandhi (tone change) in many contexts — see the Tone Sandhi section below

### 4. 第四声 (去声) à — Falling Tone (51)

Sharp, decisive drop from high to low.

- **Vietnamese comparison:** Similar to Vietnamese **thanh nặng** (heavy, short, falling like "mạ")
- **Example:** 骂 mà (scold)
- **How to produce:** Start high and drop sharply — like saying "Không!" firmly in Vietnamese

### 5. 轻声 (neutral) — Light Tone

Light, short, and unstressed. No distinct pitch contour.

- **Vietnamese comparison:** No direct equivalent — think of it as a shortened, weakened syllable
- **Example:** 妈妈 māma (mom — the second "ma" is neutral tone)
- **How to produce:** Say it quickly and softly, without a clear tone pattern

## Vietnamese ↔ Mandarin Tone Mapping

Vietnamese is also a tonal language with 6 tones. Understanding the mapping helps Vietnamese students intuitively grasp Mandarin tones.

| Vietnamese Tone | Pitch Pattern | Closest Mandarin Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Thanh ngang (a) | Mid-level | Tone 1 (ā) | ma → 妈 mā |
| Thanh huyền (à) | Low-falling | Tone 4 (à) | ma → 骂 mà |
| Thanh hỏi (ả) | Mid-dipping | Tone 3 (ǎ) | ma → 马 mǎ |
| Thanh ngã (ã) | Glottal-rise | Tone 2 (á) | Different sound, similar contour |
| Thanh sắc (á) | High-rising | Tone 2 (á) | Similar rising pattern |
| Thanh nặng (ạ) | Low-falling short | Tone 4 (à) | Similar sharp fall |

**Key insight for Vietnamese students:** Vietnamese **thanh ngang** maps to Mandarin Tone 1, and Vietnamese **thanh nặng** maps to Mandarin Tone 4. These are the most natural mappings. Tones 2 and 3 require more conscious practice.

## Vietnamese-Specific Common Mistakes

### 1. zh/ch/sh vs z/c/s — Retroflex vs Dental Sounds

Vietnamese lacks retroflex sounds, so students often substitute z/c/s for zh/ch/sh.

- **zh [ʈʂ]:** 知 zhī (know) — tongue curled back toward the roof of the mouth
- **ch [ʈʂʰ]:** 吃 chī (eat) — same as zh but aspirated (puff of air)
- **sh [ʂ]:** 是 shì (is) — tongue curled, hushing sound
- **z [ts]:** 资 zī (capital) — tongue flat, near the teeth
- **c [tsʰ]:** 粗 cū (thick) — same as z but aspirated
- **s [s]:** 思 sī (think) — tongue flat, hissing sound

**Practice tip:** "Retroflex = curl tongue toward the roof of the mouth." Place your tongue behind your lower teeth and curl the tip upward. The difference between zh/sh and z/s is tongue position, not the sound itself.

**Minimal pair practice:**
- 知 zhī (know) vs 资 zī (capital)
- 吃 chī (eat) vs 吃 cī (not a word, but helps feel the difference)
- 是 shì (is) vs 四 sì (four)

### 2. j/q/x — Palato-Alveolar Sounds

Vietnamese speakers tend to add a "y" sound before j/q/x.

- **j [tɕ]:** 机 jī (machine) — NOT "ji" with a y sound, more like "jee" but with the tongue flat
- **q [tɕʰ]:** 七 qī (seven) — same as j but aspirated
- **x [ɕ]:** 西 xī (west) — like "shee" but with the tongue flatter and further forward

**Practice tip:** For j/q/x, spread your lips slightly (like a smile) and keep the tongue flat against the lower teeth. The sound should feel like it's made in the front of the mouth, not the back.

**Common error:** 西 xī → "see" (too far back) ✓ Correct: tongue flat, lips spread

### 3. ü vs u — Front Rounded Vowel

Vietnamese "u" [u] vs Chinese "ü" [y] — one of the trickiest sounds for Vietnamese speakers since both languages have "u" but only Chinese has "ü".

- **ü [y]:** 女 nǚ (female) — round your lips like "u" but say "i"
- **u [u]:** 努 nǔ (strive) — regular "u" as in Vietnamese

**Practice tip:** Start by saying "ee" (like Vietnamese "y"), then slowly round your lips into an "o" shape while maintaining the "ee" tongue position. The result is "ü".

**Minimal pair practice:**
- 女 nǚ (female) vs 努 nǔ (strive)
- 绿 lǜ (green) vs 路 lù (road)

### 4. r — The Chinese "r" Sound

Vietnamese "d" (which sounds like "y" in Southern dialect or "z" in Northern dialect) vs Chinese "r".

- **r [ʐ]:** 人 rén (person) — NOT "dân" — tongue curled slightly back, voiced
- The Chinese "r" is somewhere between the English "r" and "zh"

**Practice tip:** Start with the "zh" sound (舌面后翘), then add vocal cord vibration. It should feel buzzy. Vietnamese students should NOT substitute "d" or "gi" for "r".

**Minimal pair practice:**
- 人 rén (person) vs 仁 rén (benevolence) — same pinyin, different meaning
- 日 rì (sun/day) — practice the "r" + "i" combination

## Practice Procedures

### 1. Tone Pairs Drill

Practice minimal pairs that differ only by tone:

```
妈 mā (mom)     vs  麻 má (hemp)     vs  马 mǎ (horse)     vs  骂 mà (scold)
```

Have the student read each pair slowly, focusing on the pitch contour. Then gradually increase speed.

### 2. Tone Sandhi

Teach the most important tone change rule:

**Rule:** When two Tone 3 syllables appear together, the first changes to Tone 2.

- 你好 nǐ hǎo → ní hǎo (the first 你 changes from 3rd to 2nd tone)
- 很好 hěn hǎo → hén hǎo
- 理解 lǐ jiě → lí jiě

**Other sandhi rules:**
- Tone 3 before non-Tone 3: becomes a low tone (21, not full 214)
- 不 bù is Tone 4 normally, but becomes Tone 2 before another Tone 4: 不对 bú duì
- 一 yī changes: Tone 4 before Tone 4, Tone 2 before Tone 1/2/3

### 3. Initials Practice

Group practice by initial consonant category:

**Voiceless aspirated (p, t, k, c, ch, q, zh):** Feel the puff of air
- Hold a piece of paper in front of your mouth — it should move
- 怕 pà, 塔 tǎ, 卡 kǎ

**Voiceless unaspirated (b, d, g, z, s, j, x, sh):** No puff of air
- The paper should NOT move
- 八 bā, 大 dà, 歌 gē

### 4. Finals Practice

Practice challenging final (vowel + nasal) combinations:

**-ang endings:** 江 jiāng, 方 fāng, 光 guāng
**-eng endings:** 风 fēng, 生 shēng, 朋 péng
**-ing endings:** 明 míng, 听 tīng, 青 qīng
**-ong endings:** 中 zhōng, 龙 lóng, 同 tóng

Vietnamese students often confuse -eng and -ing. Practice distinguishing them.

### 5. Speed Reading

Gradually increase reading speed while maintaining correct tones:

1. Start slow: Read each syllable with exaggerated tones
2. Medium: Read at natural conversational pace
3. Fast: Read quickly while keeping tones accurate

Use short phrases first, then sentences, then paragraphs.

## Response Format

When teaching pronunciation, use this consistent format:

```
📖 [Word] [Pinyin with tone marks]
🔊 [Breakdown: initial + final + tone]
🇻🇳 [Vietnamese comparison if applicable]
💡 [Tip for common mistakes]
👂 [Listen and repeat suggestion]
```

**Example:**

```
📖 吃饭 chī fàn — to eat (a meal)
🔊 ch-ī (Tone 1) + f-àn (Tone 4)
🇻🇳 chī has a retroflex initial — curl tongue back, unlike Vietnamese "x"
   fàn has Tone 4 (falling) — similar to Vietnamese thanh nặng
💡 Remember: ch is retroflex (tongue curled), NOT flat like "x" in Vietnamese
👂 Repeat 3 times: chī fàn, chī fàn, chī fàn — focus on the falling tone on fàn
```

## Pitfalls

Avoid these common teaching mistakes:

- **Don't overwhelm with IPA notation** — use simple descriptions and Vietnamese comparisons instead
- **Focus on the most common mistakes first** — retroflex sounds and tones are higher priority than rare sounds
- **Always connect back to Vietnamese sounds** the student already knows — this builds on existing intuitions
- **Tone 3 in isolation vs. in sandhi contexts** is confusing — clearly distinguish when you're teaching the "real" Tone 3 vs. the sandhi-modified version
- **Vietnamese students overuse Tone 4 for emphasis** — remind them that Tone 4 is specific to certain syllables, not a general emphasis tool
- **Don't skip tone marks in pinyin** — always include them for beginners; they're essential for correct pronunciation
- **Avoid comparing Chinese tones to musical notes** — while sometimes helpful, it can mislead students about the relative nature of tones

## Verification

After teaching pronunciation, have the student demonstrate understanding by:

1. **Reading tone pairs aloud** and self-correcting (e.g., 妈 mā vs 马 mǎ)
2. **Recording themselves** (if voice mode is available) and comparing to native audio
3. **Identifying tones in new words** without prompts (e.g., "What tone is 好 in 你好?")
4. **Completing tone sandhi exercises** (e.g., "How do you actually say 你好?")
5. **Producing minimal pairs correctly** (e.g., zhī vs zī, nǚ vs nǔ)

For voice-based interactions, use the STT service to verify the student's pronunciation and provide real-time feedback on tone accuracy.
